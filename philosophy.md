  <span class="section-eyebrow">Teaching Philosophy</span>
  <h1>Clarity, challenge, and care</h1>

  <p>
    I believe that effective teaching begins with clarity, purpose, and strong relationships. Students learn best when they understand what they are learning, why it matters, and how they can improve. In mathematics, I aim to create a classroom where students feel both supported and challenged: supported enough to take risks, ask questions, and learn from mistakes, and challenged enough to think deeply, communicate clearly, and develop true independence.
  </p>

  <div class="pull-quote">
    All students can make progress in mathematics when instruction is carefully structured, expectations are high, and feedback is honest and supportive.
  </div>

  <h2>Understanding before procedure</h2>
  <p>
    My teaching is grounded in the belief that all students can make progress in mathematics when instruction is carefully structured and expectations are high. I place strong emphasis on conceptual understanding, not just answer-getting. Students should be able to explain their reasoning, justify their methods, and connect ideas across topics. Whether I am teaching middle school, high school, or IB mathematics, I work to make learning rigorous, accessible, and meaningful.
  </p>

  <h2>How lessons are designed</h2>
  <p>
    In practice, this means designing lessons that combine explicit instruction, guided practice, questioning, discussion, and purposeful problem-solving. I use examples strategically, break complex ideas into manageable steps, and gradually release responsibility so that students become more confident and independent over time. I also believe that challenge is essential. Students should regularly encounter tasks that stretch their thinking and require persistence, reflection, and precision.
  </p>

  <h2>Assessment that guides learning</h2>
  <p>
    Assessment is an important part of my teaching, not only to measure learning but to guide it. I use assessment to identify misunderstandings, provide timely feedback, and help students see the next steps in their learning. I value feedback that is specific, actionable, and growth-oriented, and I encourage students to reflect on their progress so that they become more aware of their strengths and areas for development.
  </p>

  <h2>Classroom culture</h2>
  <p>
    I also believe that a strong classroom culture matters. Students learn best in an environment where expectations are clear, routines are consistent, and respect is mutual. I aim to build a classroom atmosphere that is calm, focused, and positive, where students feel safe to contribute and where effort, curiosity, and improvement are valued.
  </p>

  <div class="pull-quote">
    Students learn best when expectations are high, routines are clear, and they feel safe to ask questions, make mistakes, and improve.
  </div>

  <h2>A reflective profession</h2>
  <p>
    Finally, I see teaching as a reflective profession. My philosophy continues to grow through experience, collaboration, and ongoing professional learning. My goal is not only to help students succeed in mathematics, but also to help them become thoughtful, resilient, and confident learners.
  </p>
</div>
